# PART I — FOUNDATIONS

## 1. Executive Summary

FINBERG ADVISORY OÜ ("Finberg" or "the Firm") is a European management consultancy headquartered in Estonia and incorporated under the EMTAK code **70201 — Business and Other Management Consultancy Activities**. The Firm is positioned to become **one of Europe's most professional independent advisory boutiques** by combining the analytical rigor of Tier-1 strategy houses with the agility, partner attention, and AI-augmented delivery of a modern digital-native firm.

**Strategic intent.** Over the next decade, Finberg will build a defensible position as the trusted strategic and operational advisor to **mid-market enterprises, founder-led businesses, family offices (in their non-investment capacities), and corporate groups** operating in the European Economic Area, with a deliberate concentration on the **Baltic–Italian–DACH corridor**.

**What we sell.** Strategy, operations, finance advisory (non-regulated), digital and AI transformation, M&A support (advisory, never brokerage), ESG and governance consulting, and tailored executive workshops. We do **not** sell, distribute, or recommend any regulated investment product.

**How we win.** Three structural advantages:

1. **Sector depth in chosen verticals** (industrial mid-cap, professional services, technology, consumer brands) rather than horizontal generalism.
2. **AI-native delivery model** — every engagement runs on a proprietary delivery stack that reduces research and synthesis time by 40–60% versus traditional consulting, allowing partners to spend more time on judgment and client relationships.
3. **Partner accountability** — every engagement has a named partner who is contractually accountable for outcomes, not just hours.

**Financial ambition.** By 2030 the Firm targets an annual revenue range that places it in the top quartile of European boutique consultancies (per market benchmarks) with EBITDA margins of 25–32% and a partner-to-staff ratio that keeps engagement quality at Tier-1 standard. By 2035 the Firm aims to be a multi-office European boutique with a sustainable book of recurring advisory relationships, optional adjacent revenue streams (training, content licensing, IP), and a structured succession plan that allows partner renewal without dilution of quality.

**Three strategic pillars** drive every decision:

- **Pillar I — Trusted Advisor Brand.** Build the most credible, thought-leading brand in our chosen segments through publication, sector authority, and impeccable delivery.
- **Pillar II — Operating Excellence.** Run the Firm as a system — measured, repeatable, continuously improved — so that quality is institutional, not personal.
- **Pillar III — Disciplined Growth.** Grow only where we can deliver excellence; refuse work that compromises brand or margin; expand geographically only when we have a partner on the ground.

This manual operationalizes those three pillars across every dimension of the Firm.

## 2. Purpose of this Manual

This Corporate Strategy Manual exists to:

1. **Encode the strategic intent** of FINBERG ADVISORY OÜ so that it survives any individual founder, partner, or executive.
2. **Align all functions** (sales, marketing, operations, finance, HR, legal, technology, AI) around a single, coherent strategic frame.
3. **Standardize strategic thinking** by providing the analytical frameworks, decision rules, and KPIs that the Firm will use to evaluate opportunities and risks.
4. **Onboard new partners, executives, and senior consultants** to the strategic logic of the Firm in days, not months.
5. **Provide investors, lenders, and acquirers** with a clear, professional view of how the Firm is built and where it is going.

This manual is the **constitution of the Firm**. Subordinate manuals (Operations, Sales, Marketing, Finance, HR, Legal, AI, Technology, Knowledge Base) operationalize specific functions but must always be consistent with this document. In any conflict between this manual and a subordinate manual, this manual prevails until the Executive Committee formally amends it.

## 5. Mission Statement

> **We help ambitious European enterprises make better decisions, execute them with discipline, and build organizations that compound value over decades — combining the analytical rigor of the world's best consulting firms with the speed, intimacy, and AI fluency of a modern boutique.**

**Components of the mission.** Every word is deliberate.

- **"Ambitious European enterprises"** — we serve clients that have a real intention to grow, transform, or institutionalize. We do not serve clients seeking to extract opinions to justify decisions already made.
- **"Better decisions"** — our first product is decision quality, not deliverables.
- **"Execute them with discipline"** — we are accountable beyond the slide deck; we follow through.
- **"Build organizations that compound value over decades"** — we think in long horizons, not engagements.
- **"Analytical rigor of the world's best consulting firms"** — Tier-1 standard of analysis is the floor, not the ceiling.
- **"Speed, intimacy, and AI fluency"** — three structural advantages over Tier-1 incumbents.
- **"Modern boutique"** — we are not, and never intend to be, a Big-4 commodity provider.

**Mission discipline.** When an opportunity, hire, investment, or partnership does not advance the mission, the default answer is "no". The mission is the most senior decision-maker in the Firm.

## 7. Core Values

The Firm's values are non-negotiable behavioral standards. They are used in hiring, promotion, performance reviews, partner admission, and conflict resolution.

### 7.1 The Seven Core Values

1. **Integrity Above Revenue.** We refuse engagements, clients, and tactics that would compromise our honesty. We tell clients what they need to hear, not what they want to hear. We will lose a contract before we lose our credibility.

2. **Client Outcomes Over Hours.** Our success is measured by client outcomes, not by hours billed. We design engagements around impact, not effort.

3. **Excellence as Standard, Not Aspiration.** Every deliverable, every meeting, every email represents the Firm. Mediocrity is not allowed to ship. The bar is set by the best people on the Firm, not the average.

4. **Ownership and Accountability.** Every engagement has a named partner who owns the outcome. Every project has a named lead. Every commitment has a name and a date. There are no orphaned decisions.

5. **Intellectual Honesty.** We argue with data, not titles. We change our minds when the facts change. We label our assumptions. We acknowledge what we do not know.

6. **Discretion and Confidentiality.** We protect our clients' information as if it were our own — and often better. Client confidentiality survives the engagement, the employment, and the firm.

7. **Long-Term Thinking.** We build for decades. We do not optimize quarterly. We invest in capability, brand, and people even when the short-term math is unfavorable, because compounding rewards patience.

### 7.2 Operationalizing the Values

| Value | Behavioral Signal | Anti-pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Integrity Above Revenue | Walking away from a client briefing where the desired outcome is predetermined | Tailoring conclusions to match client expectation |
| Client Outcomes Over Hours | Re-scoping a project when the diagnosis changes mid-engagement | Padding hours; cosmetic deliverables |
| Excellence as Standard | Killing a deck the night before delivery and rebuilding it | "It's fine, it'll do" |
| Ownership and Accountability | "I own this. I'll have it by Tuesday 16:00 CET." | "Someone should look into that." |
| Intellectual Honesty | "I was wrong; here is the corrected analysis." | Defending the original answer after the data has moved |
| Discretion and Confidentiality | Refusing to mention a client by name even at conferences without permission | Casual name-dropping; bragging |
| Long-Term Thinking | Spending three months training a new hire properly | Throwing a junior into a high-stakes engagement to save fees |

Values violations are addressed through the People Manual (Manual 06). Sustained or severe violations are grounds for termination regardless of revenue contribution.

## 9. Company Philosophy

The Firm's philosophy is the set of beliefs about *how the world of business and advisory actually works* that guides our choices.

### 9.1 Twelve Beliefs

1. **Most strategic failure is failure of execution, not failure of analysis.** Therefore, we are as serious about implementation as we are about strategy.
2. **Most consulting failure is failure of diagnosis, not failure of recommendation.** Therefore, we invest disproportionately in understanding the actual problem.
3. **Compounding beats brilliance.** A 1% weekly improvement, sustained, beats a 50% breakthrough that fades.
4. **The most valuable skill in advisory is independence of mind.** We hire and promote for it.
5. **Reputation is built in years and destroyed in minutes.** Therefore, we are conservative in claims and aggressive in delivery.
6. **The right "no" is more valuable than the wrong "yes".** Refusing the wrong client, wrong hire, or wrong engagement creates more value than accepting it would.
7. **Information asymmetry between client and consultant is unhealthy and short-lived.** We educate clients; we want them to ask harder questions next year than this year.
8. **AI is a leverage tool, not a substitute for judgment.** AI multiplies the productivity of good consultants and exposes the limitations of bad ones.
9. **Boutique scale is a feature, not a limitation.** Our size lets us care about each engagement in a way Big-4 cannot.
10. **Talent is the only sustainable asset of an advisory firm.** Therefore, talent strategy is corporate strategy.
11. **Pricing reflects value, not cost.** We price by impact bands, defended by case studies, not by hours of partner time.
12. **Honesty with the client is the highest form of service.** A client who is told the truth, even painfully, becomes a client for life.

### 9.2 How the Philosophy is Used

These beliefs are not slogans. They are decision rules. When an executive faces an ambiguous choice and no policy applies, the philosophy applies. When a junior consultant is unsure what the Firm would do, the philosophy is the answer.

## 10. The Finberg Way — Operating Principles

A short, internal codification of how work gets done day-to-day.

1. **"Diagnose before prescribing."** No recommendation is made before a structured diagnosis is documented and approved.
2. **"One slide, one message."** Every slide has a single, defensible claim in the title.
3. **"Data, then opinion."** Every claim is backed by a source, a calculation, or a labeled assumption.
4. **"Three options, not one."** Strategic recommendations are presented as options with trade-offs, not as single answers.
5. **"Partner attends."** A partner is in every client meeting. No exceptions.
6. **"Document everything client-facing."** Every meeting has minutes, every deliverable has a version log, every commitment has an owner and a date.
7. **"Two-pizza teams."** Engagement teams are kept small (3–6 people). Quality scales through senior leverage, not headcount.
8. **"24-hour rule."** Every client message is acknowledged within 24 hours, even if the substantive answer requires longer.
9. **"Friday review."** Every active engagement has a 30-minute internal quality review every Friday.
10. **"Kill your darlings."** A slide, a recommendation, or an engagement that does not serve the client outcome is removed, no matter how hard we worked on it.

## 11. PESTLE Analysis

PESTLE is a structured scan of the macro environment that shapes demand for our services, our cost base, and our risk exposure. The current assessment, with a horizon of 36 months, is summarized below.

### 11.1 Political

- **Heightened geopolitical risk in Europe.** Increased defense spending, supply-chain re-shoring, sanctions complexity, and political instability in several EU member states create persistent demand for risk advisory, geopolitical scenario planning, and supply-chain redesign.
- **EU industrial policy.** The European Chips Act, the Critical Raw Materials Act, NIS2, and the AI Act create regulatory demand drivers across multiple sectors.
- **Estonian political stability.** Estonia remains one of the most stable, business-friendly EU jurisdictions, with low corruption, high digital government maturity, and strong rule of law — a structural advantage for our domicile.

**Strategic implication.** Demand for regulatory readiness, supply chain redesign, geopolitical risk planning, and operating model resilience will remain elevated. We build specialized offerings around these themes.

### 11.2 Economic

- **Persistent inflation moderating.** Cost-of-services inflation in professional services markets remains above general CPI; we must price accordingly.
- **Higher cost of capital.** Many mid-cap clients have re-evaluated growth investments and M&A; demand shifts from "growth strategy" toward "cost transformation", "capital allocation discipline", and "selective M&A".
- **Eurozone growth differentials.** DACH region structural weakness creates restructuring and transformation demand; Southern Europe (notably Italy) has pockets of structural opportunity through PNRR-funded modernization programs.
- **Mid-market liquidity.** Family-owned mid-caps facing generational transition continue to seek advisory on succession, governance, and ownership structuring.

**Strategic implication.** Tilt the portfolio toward cost, capital, restructuring, and succession-related services over the next 24 months while preserving capacity for growth advisory as the cycle turns.

### 11.3 Social

- **Aging workforce in many European industrial sectors.** Drives demand for knowledge transfer, automation, and operating model redesign.
- **Talent expectations shift.** Top consulting talent demands flexible work, equity, mission alignment, and AI fluency. Firms that cannot offer these will lose the talent race.
- **Trust in large institutions has declined.** Independent boutiques benefit at the expense of generalist mega-firms perceived as conflicted.

**Strategic implication.** Position the Firm as the independent, partner-attentive alternative. Build a talent value proposition that wins on autonomy, equity participation, and learning velocity.

### 11.4 Technological

- **Generative AI.** Now embedded in research, drafting, synthesis, and analysis. Firms that integrate AI deeply gain 30–50% productivity uplift in research-heavy phases. Firms that do not will lose price competitiveness within 3–5 years.
- **Data platforms.** Client expectation for data-driven, real-time advisory is rising rapidly.
- **Cybersecurity.** Advisory firms holding sensitive client data are increasingly targeted. Cyber posture is now a sales prerequisite, not a back-office concern.

**Strategic implication.** AI-native delivery is a structural pillar (Pillar III). Cybersecurity is foundational and addressed in Manual 09.

### 11.5 Legal

- **GDPR maturity.** Operational discipline required; covered in Manual 07.
- **NIS2 Directive.** Drives demand for cybersecurity governance advisory in mid-cap clients.
- **EU AI Act.** Drives demand for AI governance, model risk, and responsible AI advisory.
- **MiFID II / national investment-services regimes.** We must continuously document our non-regulated status and avoid scope creep into regulated activities.

**Strategic implication.** Maintain rigorous regulatory perimeter discipline. Productize advisory offerings around NIS2 and the AI Act.

### 11.6 Environmental

- **CSRD and ESRS.** Sustainability reporting now mandatory for large EU undertakings and, by 2026–2028 stages, many mid-caps. Drives sustained demand for ESG strategy, materiality assessment, governance design, and reporting readiness.
- **Climate transition planning.** Increasingly required by clients, lenders, and insurers.

**Strategic implication.** Build a credible ESG and sustainability advisory practice that is genuinely advisory (not audit) and that complements (rather than competes with) the assurance firms.

## 12. Porter's Five Forces

### 12.1 Competitive Rivalry — High

- The European consulting market is fragmented but highly competitive.
- Tier-1 firms (McKinsey, BCG, Bain), Big-4 (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG), large independents (Roland Berger, Oliver Wyman, A.T. Kearney/Kearney, Strategy&), and a long tail of boutiques compete for similar work.
- Rivalry intensified by the entry of technology-led players (Accenture, Capgemini Invent, BCG X, McKinsey Digital) blurring strategy and implementation.
- Boutique advantage: senior attention, sector specialization, conflict-free, faster cycle time.

### 12.2 Bargaining Power of Buyers — Medium to High

- Mid-cap and family-office buyers are increasingly sophisticated, frequently running competitive RFPs.
- Buyers have access to alternatives, including in-house corporate strategy teams, freelance senior advisors, and AI-augmented research tools.
- Mitigated by trust, sector expertise, and the cost of switching mid-engagement.

### 12.3 Bargaining Power of Suppliers — Medium

- The principal "supplier" of a consulting firm is talent.
- Talent power is rising due to remote work, transparent compensation benchmarks, and the rise of independent advisory.
- Mitigated by equity participation, brand, learning velocity, and culture.
- Other suppliers (software, data, premises) are low power.

### 12.4 Threat of New Entrants — Medium

- Low capital requirements to enter consulting.
- But brand, network, and proof-points are difficult to build.
- AI-native boutiques are a real and growing threat at the low end and can move upmarket.
- Our defense: brand, sector authority, multi-year relationships, demonstrable case-study evidence.

### 12.5 Threat of Substitutes — Medium to High

- In-house strategy teams (insourced advisory).
- Independent freelance ex-consultants.
- AI-augmented research and synthesis platforms.
- Industry associations and research houses for specific deliverables.
- Our defense: integrated diagnosis-to-execution capability, named partner accountability, and proprietary frameworks substitutes cannot replicate.

### 12.6 Five Forces Score and Strategic Implications

| Force | Intensity | Strategic Response |
|---|---|---|
| Rivalry | High | Differentiate sharply on sector depth, partner attention, AI-native delivery |
| Buyer power | Medium-High | Move from project to retainer / multi-year advisory wherever credible |
| Supplier (talent) power | Medium | Equity, brand, culture, learning velocity |
| New entrants | Medium | Build switching costs through embedded relationships and proprietary IP |
| Substitutes | Medium-High | Integrate diagnosis-to-execution; productize where defensible |

**Overall industry attractiveness: Medium.** Our positioning shifts the *effective* attractiveness for Finberg to High, because our chosen sub-segments and operating model neutralize the most threatening forces.

## 14. Value Chain Analysis

A consulting firm's value chain has both primary activities (which directly create client value) and support activities (which enable them). Our value chain design is below.

### 14.1 Primary Activities

1. **Demand Generation** — thought leadership, network cultivation, content, events, referrals. Owner: Marketing + Partners. KPI: qualified opportunities per quarter.
2. **Origination and Qualification** — inbound triage, outbound, RFP response. Owner: Sales + Partners. KPI: conversion to discovery; conversion to proposal.
3. **Scoping and Proposal** — diagnosis design, scoping, pricing, contracting. Owner: Engagement Partner + CSO Office. KPI: win rate; price realization.
4. **Engagement Delivery** — diagnosis, recommendation, implementation support. Owner: Engagement Partner + Engagement Manager. KPI: NPS, on-time, on-scope, on-budget.
5. **Client Success and Renewal** — relationship management, follow-on, references. Owner: Engagement Partner + Account Director. KPI: re-engagement rate, NPS, referral generation.

### 14.2 Support Activities

1. **Firm Infrastructure** — strategy, finance, legal, compliance, governance. Owner: Executive Committee.

## 20. Buyer Personas

A buyer persona defines who actually signs the engagement letter, who influences, and who blocks.

### 20.1 Persona A — The Founder-CEO

- Title: Founder, CEO, Managing Director
- Company: €30–300M revenue, often majority-owned by the buyer

- Goals: protect what was built; create durable value; exit on own terms or pass to heirs
- Buying criteria: trust; partner attention; discretion; pragmatic; not theoretical
- Blockers: skepticism toward outside advisors; previous bad experience with Big-4

- Sales motion: long relationship-led courtship; partner-to-founder dialogue; small first engagement that earns trust

### 20.2 Persona B — The Family Principal

- Title: Family principal, family office head, family CEO
- Company: family-owned holding structure with operating businesses
- Pain: governance among family members; succession across generations; institutionalization
- Goals: protect family wealth, preserve harmony, professionalize operations
- Buying criteria: maximum discretion; partner-led; multi-generational thinking
- Blockers: anything that resembles "selling them something"
- Channels: trusted advisor network (lawyers, private bankers in non-investment introducer capacity, family-office associations)
- Sales motion: trust-led; partner introductions only; deeply discreet

### 20.3 Persona C — The Mid-Cap CFO

- Title: CFO, Group CFO
- Company: €100M–€2B revenue
- Pain: cost transformation; capital allocation discipline; investor / lender communication; ESG reporting readiness
- Goals: stabilize margins; communicate credibly to capital providers; modernize finance function
- Buying criteria: rigor, defensibility under board scrutiny, sector benchmarks
- Blockers: budget approval cycles; competing Big-4 relationships
- Channels: CFO peer networks, finance content, RFP processes
- Sales motion: structured pitch with benchmark data; reference call with peer CFOs

### 20.4 Persona D — The PE Operating Partner

- Title: Operating Partner, Portfolio Operations Director
- Pain: extracting value from portfolio companies; hitting holding-period EBITDA targets
- Goals: bankable, time-bound transformation programs
- Buying criteria: speed, predictability, partner intensity, track record
- Blockers: fund-level procurement processes; firm panels
- Channels: PE operating partner networks; sector specialists
- Sales motion: precise scoped offerings; quantified value-creation hypotheses

### 20.5 Persona E — The Strategy / Transformation Lead

- Title: Chief Strategy Officer, Chief Transformation Officer, Director of Strategy
- Pain: stretched in-house team; need credible external bench
- Goals: deliver board-mandated programs on time
- Buying criteria: collaborative posture, IP shared willingly, no political games
- Blockers: protecting territory from external advisor encroachment
- Channels: strategy professional networks
- Sales motion: position as the "second brain", not the replacement

# PART VIII — GROWTH STRATEGY

## 40. Organic Growth Levers

Six levers, applied selectively.

1. **Account expansion.** Convert successful engagements into adjacent engagements at the same client. Owner: Engagement Partner. Target: ≥ 1.5 follow-on engagements per first engagement.
2. **Sector penetration.** Deepen presence in chosen sectors via thought leadership and reference work. Owner: Sector Leaders. Target: defined per sector annually.
3. **Service line cross-sell.** Sell adjacent practices into existing accounts. Owner: Engagement Partner + Account Director. Target: ≥ 20% of revenue from cross-practice engagements by 2028.
4. **Productized assessments.** Use low-friction, fixed-fee diagnostics to seed larger engagements. Owner: Practice Leaders. Target: ≥ 8 productized engagements / year by 2027.
5. **Retainer conversion.** Move successful project clients onto retainer where appropriate. Owner: Engagement Partner. Target: ≥ 15% of clients on retainer by 2028.
6. **Pricing realization.** Annual repricing of standard offerings and stretch pricing on premium engagements. Owner: CFO + Pricing Council. Target: ≥ 3% price realization YoY ex-mix.

## 42. Service Line Expansion

### 42.1 New Service Line Test

A new service line is added only when all five tests are met:

1. **Demand evidence** — clients are asking for it.
2. **Capability evidence** — we have or can credibly hire the talent.
3. **Adjacency** — it is a natural extension of existing practices.
4. **Margin** — it can be delivered at firm-target gross margin.
5. **Brand fit** — it does not dilute our positioning.

### 42.2 Service Line Sunset

A service line is sunset when any of the following persist for two consecutive years:

- Gross margin below firm-target by more than 10 percentage points
- NPS below firm-target by more than 15 points
- Client demand declining
- Inability to recruit and retain leadership

## 48. Scenario Planning

The Firm maintains four published scenarios, refreshed annually. Each scenario has a defined strategic response.

### 48.1 Base Case (2026–2030)

Assumptions:
- European GDP growth 1–2% p.a., low to moderate inflation.
- Mid-cap demand for advisory steady, with slight tilt toward cost / restructuring.
- AI delivery becomes table stakes by 2028.

Strategic response: execute the plan as documented in Parts VII–VIII.

### 48.2 Bull Case

Assumptions:
- European GDP growth 2.5–4% p.a.; rebound in confidence.
- Surge in transformation, growth, and M&A advisory demand.
- Talent market loosens as new entrants slow.

Strategic response:
- Accelerate hiring within partner economics constraints.
- Bring forward geographic expansion by 12 months.
- Increase brand investment to capture share.
- Avoid over-hiring beyond demand visibility.

### 48.3 Bear Case

Assumptions:
- European GDP growth flat or negative; persistent inflation.
- Advisory budgets compressed 15–25%.
- Pricing pressure significant.

Strategic response:
- Tilt portfolio toward restructuring, cost, capital, and operational transformation.
- Maintain cash runway minimum at 12 months (raise threshold).
- Defer non-essential hiring.
- Preserve brand and IP investments; cut travel, optional events, non-strategic R&D first.
- Increase productized offerings to lower buying friction.

### 48.4 Black Swan (Severe Shock)

Assumptions:
- Major macro shock (financial crisis, war escalation, pandemic, energy crisis) compressing GDP 5%+.
- Several client cancellations.
- Severe pricing pressure.

Strategic response:
- Triage existing engagements; preserve high-quality work.
- Activate Crisis Strategy Playbook (Section 50).
- Cash preservation mode: defer all discretionary spending; renegotiate variable costs.
- Maintain communications with clients and team; transparency is the brand.
- Re-plan the year within 60 days of shock.

## 49. Mitigation Frameworks

### 49.1 Risk Mitigation Hierarchy

For each risk, mitigation is designed in this preference order:

1. **Avoid** — refuse the activity that creates the risk.
2. **Reduce** — engineer controls that lower likelihood or impact.
3. **Transfer** — insure or contract the risk to a third party.
4. **Accept** — when avoidance is impractical and the risk is within appetite, accept and monitor.

### 49.2 Mitigation Owners

Every risk in the register has a single named owner accountable for mitigation. Owners report on mitigation status quarterly to the Executive Committee.

### 49.3 Risk-Driven Investment Logic

Significant investments (above thresholds defined in Manual 05) include an explicit risk assessment in the business case.

## 50. Crisis Strategy Playbook

When a Black Swan or severe single-client / single-event crisis occurs.

### 50.1 Activation

The CEO (or designated deputy) declares a Crisis State. A Crisis Cell (CEO, COO, CFO, CLO, CSO, CMO) convenes within 24 hours.

### 50.2 First 72 Hours

- Stabilize: confirm safety of people, integrity of systems, contractual standing.
- Communicate internally: clear, frequent, honest team updates.
- Communicate externally: hold-position statements as required; named partners contact key clients personally.
- Establish a daily Crisis Cell call cadence.

### 50.3 First 30 Days

- Diagnose root cause via structured post-mortem.
- Quantify impact across financial, reputational, operational dimensions.
- Re-plan the next 90 days.
- Update Risk Register and Scenario Plan.

### 50.4 First 90 Days

- Implement the re-plan.
- Communicate transparently with team, clients, board.
- Embed permanent learnings into BOS.

### 50.5 Crisis Discipline

Three things are never acceptable in crisis:
1. Cutting corners on integrity or compliance.
2. Hiding bad news from the team.
3. Damaging a client to protect ourselves.

## 58. Strategic KPIs (Composite)

### 58.1 Top 10 KPIs

1. **Annualized revenue from re-engaged clients** (North Star).
2. Engagement NPS.
3. Revenue.
4. EBITDA margin %.
5. Re-engagement rate.
6. Voluntary talent turnover.

8. Regulatory perimeter incidents (zero target).
9. Cyber incidents (zero target).
10. Sector authority score (composite — Section 58.2).

### 58.2 Sector Authority Score

A composite metric per chosen sector, recalculated quarterly. Components:

- # of published pieces of original research in sector (year).
- # of unprompted mentions of Finberg in sector media.
- # of inbound inquiries from sector.
- # of speaking invitations at sector flagship events.
- # of clients in sector.

Each component is normalized and weighted. Sectors are ranked internally and tracked over time.

## 59. KPI Definitions and Calculation Standards

To avoid measurement disputes, each KPI has:

- A precise mathematical definition.
- A data source.
- An owner.
- A calculation frequency.
- A reporting cadence.
- A "tolerance band" within which deviation is normal.

The full KPI Dictionary is maintained by the CFO Office (Manual 05).
